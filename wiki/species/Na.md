# Na

Atomic sodium; an alkali metal detected in the optical/near-infrared transmission spectra of hot Jupiters. The broad Na doublet at ~589 nm is a characteristic hot-Jupiter diagnostic probing the upper atmosphere. Detected in WASP-39 b by the original ERS team (not yet ingested) and confirmed across multiple independent reductions in [[2026_RoyPerez_WASP39b]].

## Papers

- [[2026_RoyPerez_WASP39b]] — confirmed detection in WASP-39 b across independent reductions of the NIRSpec PRISM ERS dataset.
- [[2025_Verma_HD209458b]] — first robust JWST-era confirmation of Na in HD 209458 b (originally detected by Charbonneau et al. 2002 with HST/STIS; not ingested); detection significance 6.9σ from STIS+WFC3+NIRCam free-chemistry SANSAR retrieval; log VMR ≈ −5.67.
- [[2025_Deka_WASP39b]] — Na constrained on WASP-39 b panchromatic NIRISS+PRISM+MIRI data in [[NEXOTRANS]] hybrid model; log VMR ≈ −7.97 to −6.62 across Bayesian and ML retrievals; relatively low Na for a hot Jupiter consistent with high-altitude condensate formation.
- [[2026_Radica_WASP96b]] — detected in WASP-96 b panchromatic transmission spectrum; driven primarily by the archival VLT/FORS2 optical data; log VMR ≈ −4.29 to −3.53.
- [[2013_Pont_HD189733b]] — narrow Na D core detection (~4σ; Huitson 2012, not ingested) on [[HD-189733b]]; **broad pressure-broadened wings absent**, consistent with either alkali condensation onto KCl/Na₂S sulfides at the cool terminator or aerosol obscuration above the alkali line-forming pressures.
- [[2008_Pont_HD189733b]] — first non-detection of Na on HD 189733 b in HST/ACS G800L transmission; flux excess in 15-nm Na band: −6.9 ± 7.9 × 10⁻⁵ — used to argue for high-altitude aerosols obscuring the alkali doublet wings.
- [[2001_Brown_TransmissionDiagnostics]] — Brown 2001 spectrum-ratio framework; treats Na/K photoionization by stellar UV; predicts Na D line core depth ~10⁻³ relative to stellar continuum on HD 209458 b.
- [[2000_Seager_TheoryTransmission]] — **the** foundational prediction: Na I D resonance doublet at 589.4 nm is the strongest expected transmission feature for close-in giant planets; motivated the historic Charbonneau et al. 2002 first-detection (not yet ingested).
