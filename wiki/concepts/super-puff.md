# Super-puff

A super-puff is a low-mass exoplanet (M_p < 30 M⊕) with extreme low bulk density (ρ_p < 0.3 g/cm³), typically much cooler than inflated hot Jupiters (T_eq < 1000 K). Planetary structure models suggest a substantial H/He envelope > 20% of the total mass is required to inflate the radius — far more than Uranus or Neptune (~15% H/He). Most super-puffs reside in multi-planet near-resonant systems, consistent with formation in cool, dust-poor regions of the protoplanetary disc (typically beyond the water snowline) followed by gentle disc migration inward, with halt mechanisms protecting against subsequent envelope loss.

Super-puffs challenge core-accretion theory because the small cores (well below the canonical 8–10 M⊕ runaway-accretion threshold) appear to have accreted massive gas envelopes nonetheless. Three classes of explanation for the apparent low density have been proposed:

1. **Substantial H/He envelope with high-altitude photochemical haze** — the most directly observable signature is a sloped continuum in transmission spectroscopy; tholin / soot / sulfur are candidate haze compositions (Kawashima 2019, Ohno & Tanaka 2021, Gao & Zhang 2020; not ingested).
2. **Dusty outflow / mass loss** — entrained dust at high altitudes can produce a flat optical-to-near-IR spectrum and ingress/egress shape distortions (Wang & Dai 2019, Gao & Zhang 2020).
3. **Tilted ring system** — an optically thick or thin ring with submicron porous particles can mimic an inflated radius (Akinsanmi 2020, Piro & Vissapragada 2020, Ohno & Fortney 2022). Massive opaque rings produce flat spectra; thin sloped rings can mimic photochemical-haze slopes but face Pointing–Robertson lifetime constraints (∼0.1 Myr for submicron particles).

The **slow rotation** of super-puffs (e.g., Kepler-51 d > 33 hr; Lammers & Winn 2024, Liu et al. 2024) is also a formation diagnostic — gas accretion onto a small core should produce near-breakup rotation (Ormel 2015), so observed slow rotation implies either low angular-momentum accretion or a spin-down mechanism beyond tides (which are weak at the orbital distances involved).

## Distinction from related categories

- **Sub-Neptune** (radius 1.7–4 R⊕, often around mid-to-late M dwarfs) — different mass range and host-star type; generally not as low-density.
- **Inflated hot Jupiter** (M > 0.3 MJ, T_eq > 1000 K) — much hotter; inflation likely thermal/tidal rather than core-accretion-related.
- **Warm Saturn / sub-Saturn** — partially overlapping at the high-mass end of the super-puff regime; super-puff usage typically reserves "super-puff" for the most extreme densities (ρ < 0.1 g/cm³).
