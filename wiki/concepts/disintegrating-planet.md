# Disintegrating planet

A class of ultra-short-period rocky planet undergoing active mass loss driven by intense stellar irradiation, producing dust and gas tails that create asymmetric, transit-depth-variable, and wavelength-dependent transit signatures. First recognized from Kepler photometry (KIC 12557548 b; Rappaport et al. 2012; not yet ingested): the transit depth varies between 0% and ~1.3% orbit-to-orbit because the dust cloud column changes stochastically. The forward scattering of stellar light by the dust tail can produce pre-transit brightening, and the trailing tail causes a longer post-transit ingress relative to egress.

Key observable properties:
- **Stochastic transit depth variability** — sign of episodic mass-loss; non-detections are expected and informative.
- **Wavelength-dependent transit depth** — dust grain size and composition determine the spectral slope; mid-infrared observations with MIRI probe the same dust cloud with reduced stellar contrast.
- **Composition diagnostics** — vaporizing rock exposes interior mineral composition; escaping gas species (e.g., NO) trace high-temperature vaporization chemistry.

Disintegrating planets are therefore probes of rocky-planet interior composition through their ejecta, distinct from conventional [[transmission-spectroscopy]] that probes a retained atmosphere.
