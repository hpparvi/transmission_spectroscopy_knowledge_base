# JWST COMPASS: The First Near- to Mid-infrared Transmission Spectrum of the Hot Super-Earth L 168-9 b

**Authors:** Alam, Gao, Adams Redai, Wallack, Wogan, et al. (2025, AJ 169:15) · [doi:10.3847/1538-3881/ad8eb5]
**Target:** [[L168-9b]] · **Host:** [[L168-9]] · **Instruments:** [[JWST-NIRSpec]] (G395H), [[JWST-MIRI]] (LRS)
**Program:** [[JWST-GO-2512-COMPASS]] (NIRSpec) + COM 1033 commissioning (MIRI)

## TL;DR
The first broadband 3–12 μm transmission spectrum of a rocky exoplanet, combining three JWST NIRSpec/G395H transits of the hot super-Earth L 168-9 b (Teq=981 K, Rp=1.39 R⊕) with a single MIRI/LRS transit taken during instrument commissioning. The combined spectrum is **featureless**; a grid of 1D thermochemical-equilibrium forward models rules out atmospheric metallicities **< 100× solar** at > 3σ for cloudless atmospheres at P ≥ 1 bar. Photoevaporation models show L 168-9 b could not have retained a primordial H/He atmosphere beyond the first ~200 Myr for any of the tested initial-atmosphere-mass fractions (2–100%). The simplest current picture: L 168-9 b is consistent with a **bare rock or a thin high-MMW secondary atmosphere**; a follow-up MIRI eclipse at 15 μm is proposed as the path to identifying a CO₂-dominated atmosphere if one exists.

## Key findings
- Median transit-depth precision of 20 ppm across three NIRSpec G395H visits (54 R~100 channels) and 61 ppm for the MIRI/LRS visit (48 ~0.15 μm bins across 5–12 μm).
- NIRSpec + MIRI weighted-mean spectrum is flat; no atmospheric absorption features from [[H2O]], [[CH4]], [[CO2]], or high-metallicity thermochemical-equilibrium atmospheres.
- Photochemical / mass-loss modeling: initial atmospheric mass fractions from 2% to 100% all fully stripped within ~200 Myr given L 168-9's XUV history.
- MIRI/LRS achieves ~1.3× PandExo-predicted precision across NRS1 & NRS2; demonstrates MIRI's sensitivity for small-planet reconnaissance.
- Future: a MIRI secondary-eclipse observation at 15 μm is the recommended next step to identify a CO₂-dominated atmosphere if one exists.

## Methods
- **Observations:** three NIRSpec/G395H transits (2023-06-03, 2023-06-18, 2023-06-27) and one MIRI/LRS transit (2022-05-29) of L 168-9 b.
- **Reduction:** three independent NIRSpec reductions — Aesop, [[Tiberius]], [[Eureka]] — plus an ExoTiC-MIRI reduction for the MIRI/LRS data.
- **Coadding:** weighted mean across the three NIRSpec visits; no offsets applied between NRS1/NRS2 — see [[multi-visit-stacking]].
- **Forward modeling:** [[CHIMERA]] thermochemical-equilibrium grids (1–1000× solar) with the Guillot (2010) T–P profile and [[PICASO]] radiative transfer; pure single-gas H₂O, CH₄, CO₂ atmospheres also tested. See [[transmission-spectroscopy]].
- **Mass-loss modeling:** photoevaporation models with initial H/He fractions of 2–100% following Owen & Jackson 2012 / Rogers & Owen 2021; see [[cosmic-shoreline]].

## Caveats & limitations
- Absolute flux of the MIRI/LRS visit depends on commissioning-era calibrations; systematic uncertainty folded into error rescaling.
- Forward modeling assumes cloudless atmospheres; high-altitude aerosols could mute features and relax the metallicity constraint.
- The MIRI/LRS transit is a single visit; repeatability claims rest on the three NIRSpec visits.
- Interpretation via forward-model χ² comparison; no full Bayesian retrieval presented.

## Open questions / follow-ups
- Is L 168-9 b's atmosphere CO₂-dominated? Testable with MIRI secondary-eclipse at 15 μm.
- How does L 168-9 b's atmospheric inventory compare to other hot super-Earths like 55 Cnc e (Hu et al. 2024; not yet ingested)?
- Can stacking more transits reach the < 10 ppm precision needed for thin CO₂ detection at NIRSpec wavelengths?
