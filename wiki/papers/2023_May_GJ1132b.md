# Double Trouble: Two Transits of the Super-Earth GJ 1132 b Observed with JWST NIRSpec G395H

**Authors:** May, MacDonald, Bennett, Moran, Wakeford, et al. (2023, ApJL 959:L9) · [doi:10.3847/2041-8213/ad054f]
**Target:** [[GJ1132b]] · **Host:** [[GJ1132]] · **Instrument:** [[JWST-NIRSpec]] (G395H mode, 2.8–5.2 μm)
**Program:** [[JWST-GO-1981-CHAMPs]]

## TL;DR
Two JWST NIRSpec/G395H transits of the M-dwarf super-Earth GJ 1132 b, taken 8 days apart, yield **statistically discrepant transmission spectra**. Visit 1 disfavors a flat spectrum and is best fit by an [[H2O]]-dominated atmosphere with trace [[CH4]] (~1%) and [[N2O]] (~100 ppm) *or* by unocculted starspots — the data cannot distinguish these. Visit 2 is statistically consistent with a **featureless spectrum**. The authors' preferred interpretation is that Visit 1's apparent features are an **unlucky random noise draw**, since neither stellar variability nor instrumental systematics plausibly account for the visit-to-visit difference. The paper is a cautionary tale about claiming atmospheric detections on small rocky planets from single JWST visits.

Independently of the noise-vs-atmosphere question, both visits **rule out** the H₂-dominated atmosphere with [[HCN]]-like features previously inferred from HST/WFC3 by Swain et al. 2021 (not yet ingested), aligning with the featureless HST results of Mugnai et al. 2021 and Libby-Roberts et al. 2022 (not yet ingested).

## Key findings
- Visit 1 (2023 Feb 25) is inconsistent with a flat spectrum at **~2.9σ** under the [[Eureka]] reduction; Visit 2 (2023 Mar 5) is consistent with flat at ~0.1σ.
- Three independent reductions — [[Eureka]], FIREFLy, ExoTiC-JEDI — agree within each visit but reproduce the disagreement between visits, ruling out pipeline-driven systematics.
- [[POSEIDON]] retrievals on Visit 1 favor an **[[H2O]]-dominated atmosphere with ~1% [[CH4]] and ~100 ppm [[N2O]]** (Bayes factor 6.5 / 2.5σ vs no atmosphere) or, alternatively, ~20% coverage by unocculted starspots ~400 K below the photosphere — the two scenarios have indistinguishable Bayesian evidence.
- Out-of-transit PHOENIX [[stellar-contamination-modeling]] finds negligible change in spot/facula coverage between the two visits, making stellar variability an unlikely driver of the spectral difference.
- NRS1/NRS2 detector offsets, superbias systematics, and pipeline choices were tested and found **insufficient** to explain the visit-to-visit mismatch.
- Rules out a long-lived H₂-dominated atmosphere (>2.5σ in Visit 1 even at 1000× solar metallicity) and in particular the HCN-bearing H₂ atmosphere claimed from HST/WFC3 by Swain et al. 2021.
- Any atmospheric interpretation of Visit 1 faces a stability problem: at Teq ≈ 529 K an H₂O+CH₄+N₂O mixture is vulnerable to photolysis and escape, requiring a large initial H₂O inventory (>5 wt%) plus ongoing outgassing.

## Methods
- **Observations:** JWST-NIRSpec G395H, 2.8–5.2 μm, R ≈ 2700, two transits (2023-02-25 and 2023-03-05) from [[JWST-GO-1981-CHAMPs]]. Each observation: 3.06 hr, 814 integrations, 14 groups up the ramp.
- **Data reduction:** three independent pipelines — [[Eureka]] (with custom group-level background subtraction for NIRSpec 1/f noise), FIREFLy, and ExoTiC-JEDI. Light curves fit with `batman` (Kreidberg 2015) using [[transmission-spectroscopy]] conventions and `ExoTiC-LD` limb-darkening.
- **Forward modeling:** transmission models generated with CHIMERA (thermochemical equilibrium, 100–1000× solar) and PICASO (radiative transfer), binned to R ≈ 100.
- **Retrievals:** [[atmospheric-retrievals]] performed separately per visit with [[POSEIDON]] (15-parameter atmosphere model, PyMultiNest, 2000 live points). A separate four-parameter unocculted-starspot retrieval was run per visit — see [[stellar-contamination-modeling]].
- **Consistency tests:** flat-line vs Gaussian-feature Bayesian model comparison (Dynesty); χ²ν distributions estimated from 100,000 synthetic featureless spectra per visit.

## Caveats & limitations
- The atmosphere-vs-starspot degeneracy in Visit 1 cannot be broken with G395H alone; shorter-wavelength observations (λ < 3 μm, e.g. NIRISS SOSS) are required.
- A possible NRS1/NRS2 detector offset erodes but does not eliminate Visit 1's statistical significance; the authors argue a superbias correction is not warranted here.
- The H₂O+CH₄+N₂O atmosphere from Visit 1 is thermodynamically and photochemically fragile — if real, it demands substantial volatile delivery and continuous outgassing.
- Retrievals were run only on the [[Eureka]] reduction; the paper argues consistency across pipelines justifies this, but posteriors formally inherit Eureka-specific choices.

## Open questions / follow-ups
- Is Visit 1's nonflat spectrum atmospheric or stellar? Resolvable with NIRISS SOSS or other λ < 3 μm follow-up.
- Are multi-visit discrepancies of this amplitude common for small planets with G395H? The paper calls for similar repeat-visit studies across the CHAMPs sample.
- Should future small-planet programs prefer NIRSpec G395M over G395H on faint M-dwarf hosts, to avoid the NRS1/NRS2 gap and possible detector offsets?
- Does the GJ 1132 b result generalize to other CHAMPs targets (LHS 475 b, GJ 486 b already published; two others pending)?

## Tensions
- **HCN / H₂-dominated atmosphere claim:** Swain et al. 2021 (HST/WFC3; not yet ingested) reported an H₂-dominated atmosphere on GJ 1132 b with HCN and CH₄ features near 1.53 μm. Both JWST visits in this paper are inconsistent with that atmosphere at > 4.2σ across all three reductions. Southworth et al. 2017 (ground-based, H₂/H₂O/CH₄) is likewise ruled out. Mugnai et al. 2021 and Libby-Roberts et al. 2022 (HST, featureless) are consistent with the JWST data.
