# Detection of atmospheric haze on an extrasolar planet: the 0.55–1.05 μm transmission spectrum of HD 189733b with the Hubble Space Telescope

**Authors:** Pont, Knutson, Gilliland, Moutou, Charbonneau 2008, MNRAS 385:109 · [arXiv:0712.1374]
**Targets:** [[HD189733b]] · **Instrument:** [[HST-ACS]]

## TL;DR
Three [[HST-ACS]] G800L grism transits of HD 189733 b covering 550–1050 nm at S/N 4 000–10 000 per 50 nm passband per exposure (675 spectra total). The transmission spectrum is **almost featureless** at the level of a 150 km atmospheric scale height — no [[Na]] D doublet, no [[K]] doublet, no broad [[H2O]] features expected from a clear-atmosphere model (Tinetti et al. 2007; not yet ingested). The mean visible transit radius sits ~2 000 km **above** the cloud-free model and ~1 000 km above the 2–8 μm Spitzer/NICMOS infrared radius, with a smooth ~470 km redward slope across 600–1000 nm. Pont et al. interpret the combination — flatness, IR–visible offset, and redward slope — as a **haze of submicrometre condensate grains** in the upper atmosphere, blocking the grazing starlight before it can reach altitudes where Na, K, and water bands form. **First conclusive aerosol detection in an exoplanet atmosphere.**

## Key findings
- **Featureless visible spectrum.** No detection of Na 589 nm or K 769 nm; 3σ upper limits on flux excess in 15-nm bands centred on each line: −6.9 ± 7.9 × 10⁻⁵ (Na), 1.6 ± 5.2 × 10⁻⁵ (K). The 4σ Charbonneau et al. 2002 (not yet ingested) Na detection on [[HD209458b]] used a 1.2-nm band; their 10-nm band gave (3.1 ± 3.6) × 10⁻⁵ — consistent with the HD 189733 b non-detection at this resolution.
- **Redward slope.** 472 ± 42 (random) ± 106 (syst) km transit radius decrease across 400 nm of 600–1000 nm — characteristic of Rayleigh-like scattering from sub-μm aerosols.
- **IR-vs-visible offset.** Visible transit radius ~1 000 km higher than 2 μm NICMOS and 8 μm Spitzer IRAC measurements (Knutson et al. 2007) — only a wavelength-dependent absorber/scatterer at altitude can produce this.
- **Stellar contamination addressed.** The K-dwarf host is photometrically variable at the few-percent level (~12 d rotation, Croll et al. 2007; Henry & Winn 2008). Five independent reductions (Solutions A–E) using different visit selections, occulted-spot treatments, and limb-darkening choices yield consistent results. Pont et al. estimate the unocculted "background" spot level at 0–3 % (their reference solution adopts 1 %), with spot temperatures ~4 000–4 500 K from chromatic occulted-spot fitting. Concludes that even a "worst-case" 100 %-spotted-stellar-surface scenario would shift the radius by ≤500 km without altering the global featureless shape — ruling out unocculted spots as the dominant cause of the slope at this point.
- **Concluding remark:** "*The sunset over HD 189733b is red.*"

## Methods
HST/ACS G800L grism, 550–1050 nm, 8 nm resolution element. Three transits (HST programs across 2006). White-light transit fit (Paper I = Pont et al. 2007, A&A; not ingested) constrained orbital parameters. Wavelength-dependent radii fit per 50-nm passband (10 bins) and per 3-nm column (173 bins) using Mandel & Agol 2002 transit shapes with non-linear Claret 2000 four-parameter limb darkening from Kurucz models. Decorrelation against six telescope/detector vectors (x/y position, spectrum width/angle, HST orbital phase, linear time). Occulted-spot correction via empirical chromatic linear-rise fit (Visit 1 large spot crossing); unocculted-spot correction via 1 % flux-dimming assumption with chromatic dependence from cool-stellar-surface modeling. See [[transmission-spectroscopy]], [[stellar-contamination-modeling]].

## Caveats & limitations
- Single-instrument coverage; broader-wavelength interpretation depends on the Knutson et al. 2007 NICMOS/Spitzer comparison.
- Unocculted-spot level estimated from MOST + ground-based monitoring with assumed 4 000 K spot temperature; uncertainty in this temperature non-linearly affects the inferred slope (open squares vs. solid squares in their Fig. 2 show the difference between 4 000 K and 3 500 K is at the ~150 km level).
- Spectrum integrated over the day–night terminator; weather variability between transits not directly probed.
- Reflection/emission spectrum implications (cloud opacity at perpendicular vs. grazing geometry) treated heuristically.

## Open questions / follow-ups
- What is the composition of the haze? Pont et al. mention silicates, iron, Cr, MnS, photochemical products as candidates; defer detailed composition to follow-up.
- Does the haze cover the full panchromatic range, or break in some IR window?
- How does the haze interact with the 3.6 μm phase-curve hot-spot offset and emission spectrum measurements?

These three questions are taken up directly by [[2013_Pont_HD189733b]].
