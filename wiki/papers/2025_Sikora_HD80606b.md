# Seasonal Changes in the Atmosphere of HD 80606 b Observed with JWST's NIRSpec/G395H

**Authors:** Sikora, Rowe, Splinter, Barat, Dang et al. (2025, AJ 170:105)
**Targets:** [[HD80606b]] · **Instrument:** [[JWST-NIRSpec]] G395H · **Program:** [[JWST-GO-2488]] (PI: J. T. Sikora)

## TL;DR

A 20.9-hour JWST NIRSpec G395H partial-phase-curve observation of the highly eccentric hot Jupiter [[HD80606b]] (e = 0.93, P = 111.4 d, M = 4.16 M_J, R = 1.03 R_J), capturing the secondary eclipse and the immediate periapse passage on 2022 November 1. As the planet sweeps from near-apoastron (T_eq ≈ 300 K) through periapse (T_eq ≈ 1500 K), the dayside atmosphere transitions from a featureless blackbody (T_bright ≈ 892 K pre-eclipse) to a chemical-equilibrium spectrum with **CH₄, H₂O, CO₂, and (in some retrievals) CO absorption features** post-periapse, peaking at T_bright = 1313 K and then decaying. CH₄ is detected at **4.1–10.7σ** in NRS1 across post-periapse phases (depending on whether an NRS1 detector flux offset is included). H₂O at 4.2–5.5σ and CO₂ at 3.7–4.4σ. **No evidence for a strong temperature inversion** is found despite GCM predictions for HD 80606 b's atmosphere — limits the radiative timescale and cloud properties at the IR photosphere. First atmospheric demonstration that partial-phase-curve NIRSpec observations of eccentric hot Jupiters are scientifically productive.

## Key findings

- **Time-variable emission spectrum.** The first six observed phases (~10.6 hr pre-eclipse → 1.4 hr post-eclipse) are statistically indistinguishable from a blackbody at successively higher T_bright (892 K → 1216 K → 1313 K). The last four phases (1.4 → 7.4 hr post-periapse) show **chemical-equilibrium spectral features** with ΔBIC = -6 to -112 vs. blackbody.
- **CH₄ detection at 4.1–10.7σ** in NRS1 post-periapse. The high-σ end requires excluding an NRS1 detector flux-offset parameter; with the offset included, CH₄ remains > 4σ. CH₄ absorption is most prominent in the 3.0–3.5 μm window.
- **H₂O at 4.2–5.5σ** and **CO₂ at 3.7–4.4σ** in NRS2 (3.7–4.4 μm region).
- **CO present in chemical-equilibrium retrievals** but free-chemistry retrievals show CO/CH₄ ratio increasing post-periapse, qualitatively consistent with rapid CH₄ → CO conversion as the dayside heats.
- **No strong temperature inversion.** Free-retrieval T-p profiles and chemical-equilibrium retrievals both consistent with **monotonically decreasing T with altitude** at the IR photosphere; the GCM prediction (Tsai et al. 2023b; not ingested) of a transient ~400 K inversion at periapse is not supported by these data.
- **Photochemistry not required.** chemical-equilibrium retrievals favored over free-chemistry by Bayes factors at all post-periapse phases — the data don't demand disequilibrium HCN, C₂H₂, or comparable photochemical products.
- **Phase-curve amplitudes:** c1 ≈ 433 ppm at 2.8–3.7 μm (NRS1) → 813 ppm at 3.8–5.2 μm (NRS2). Ratio consistent with the larger NRS2 wavelength region carrying more thermal emission.
- **Eclipse midpoint discrepancy:** ~28 ± 4 minutes earlier than predicted from the K. A. Pearson 2022 ephemeris — possibly an underestimated uncertainty in the published T_e or a small subdued effect at large eccentricity.
- **NRS2 amplitude vs Spitzer 4.5 μm:** Sikora's NRS2 (3.83–5.17 μm) phase-curve amplitude 813 ± 7 ppm is close to but slightly higher than de Wit et al. 2016 Spitzer 4.5 μm amplitude 738 ± 52 ppm; consistent within 1.4σ.
- **Methodological message:** Partial-phase-curve observations of eccentric hot Jupiters with JWST/NIRSpec G395H are productive — even with only ~20% phase coverage, time-resolved spectra capture the radiative response.

## Methods

Single 20.9-hr JWST NIRSpec G395H BOTS observation under [[JWST-GO-2488]] on 2022 November 1, with two ~5-min interruptions for High Gain Antenna (HGA) moves. SUB2048 subarray, 5 groups/integration; 13,818 integrations. Two independent reductions: customized [[Eureka]] (v1.1) with `super_sample = 3` for centroid stability and a step-function correction for the first HGA-induced flux jump in NRS1; and a fully custom pipeline using difference-imaging photometry. Both reductions agree on the chemistry and phase-curve trends.

Light-curve model: 5-component model — F0 (constant stellar flux), Fp (asymmetric Lorentzian planet phase curve, Lewis et al. 2013), Fsin (sinusoidal residual at f1 = 0.174 hr⁻¹ NRS1 / 0.231 hr⁻¹ NRS2), Fsys (linear systematics + HGA jump correction), Fpoly (2nd-order polynomial trend). emcee MCMC with ~10⁵ samples per parameter chain.

Atmospheric inference at 10 phase bins:
- **[[petitRADTRANS]] + [[easyCHEM]]** chemical-equilibrium retrievals (60-layer atmosphere; H₂O, CO, CO₂, CH₄ opacities + H₂ Rayleigh; gray cloud deck; Guillot 2010 4-parameter T-p; emcee 16 walkers × 10⁵ steps).
- **[[PyratBay]]** free-chemistry retrievals (uniform abundances; same priors; MC3 snooker differential-evolution MCMC × 4 × 10⁶ samples).

Per-phase brightness temperatures derived by χ² fitting blackbodies to the F_p/F_★ measurements using a PHOENIX (T_eff = 5600 K, log g = 4.5, [M/H] = 0.5) stellar reference.

## Caveats & limitations

- **Partial phase curve only.** ~20% of the orbit observed; the apoastron / dayside-cooling regime not captured. Spitzer 4.5 μm phase curve provides longer-timescale context but at much lower SNR.
- **HGA-jump correction in NRS1** absorbs ~77 ppm of the planetary signal near the eclipse; mitigated by the step-function model but contributes ~4σ uncertainty to derived c1 and c3 phase-curve parameters.
- **Eclipse midpoint -28 ± 4 minutes early** vs published ephemeris — possibly an underestimated published uncertainty rather than a real planetary signal.
- **CH₄ detection significance varies (4.1–10.7σ)** depending on whether NRS1 flux offset is included; the lower bound is the more conservative reading.
- **No formal stellar-contamination retrieval.** HD 80606 is a quiet G5V (T_eff = 5565 K); contamination expected to be small but not modeled.
- **GCM comparison qualitative.** Tsai et al. 2023b GCM predicts both a transient inversion and large CH₄ → CO conversion. The first prediction is rejected here; the second is qualitatively consistent but quantitatively uncertain.
- **Single visit.** All findings rest on one orbital periapse passage. Repeat observations would test whether any of the time-variability is genuinely periodic vs. visit-specific (e.g., a transient cloud event).

## Open questions / follow-ups

- **Does a second periapse-passage observation reproduce the time-resolved chemistry?** A repeat visit would distinguish stable seasonal behavior from one-time events.
- **What's the rotation period?** The energy-conserving model of de Wit et al. 2016 derives P_rot ≈ 93 hr; pseudo-synchronous prediction is ~40 hr. A multi-visit phase curve could measure rotation directly via the asymmetric Lorentzian timescales.
- **Disequilibrium chemistry signals at lower abundance?** Although chemical equilibrium is statistically preferred, residual HCN or C₂H₂ at < 3σ could be present and would test the Tsai et al. 2023b photochemistry predictions.
- **MIRI/LRS or NIRCam follow-up.** Extending wavelength coverage would constrain the cloud properties (MgSiO₃, MnS, Na₂S predicted by Lewis et al. 2017; not ingested) and disentangle continuum vs absorption.
- **Long-baseline phase coverage.** Combining JWST partial phase curves at periapse with Spitzer-era full phase curves would yield the radiative + cloud-evolution timescale at higher precision.
