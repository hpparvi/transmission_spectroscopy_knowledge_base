# The prevalence of dust on the exoplanet HD 189733b from Hubble and Spitzer observations

**Authors:** Pont, Sing, Gibson, Aigrain, Henry, Husnoo 2013, MNRAS 432:2917 · [arXiv:1210.4163]
**Targets:** [[HD-189733b]] · **Instruments:** [[HST-STIS]], [[HST-ACS]], [[HST-WFC3]], [[HST-NICMOS]], [[Spitzer-IRAC]], [[Spitzer-MIPS]]

## TL;DR
Combines all space-based transmission observations of HD 189733 b — HST/STIS (290–637 nm) + HST/ACS (550–1050 nm) + HST/WFC3 (1.10–1.69 μm) + HST/NICMOS (1.4–2.5 μm) + Spitzer/IRAC (3.6/4.5/5.8/8.0 μm) + Spitzer/MIPS (24 μm) — onto a single coherent 0.3–24 μm scale via careful re-derivation of orbital parameters, [Hayek 2012] limb darkening, and **detailed unocculted + occulted [[stellar-contamination]] corrections**. The combined spectrum shows a near-monotonic Rayleigh-like blueward radius increase across the full UV-to-NIR range — only narrow [[Na]] D and [[K]] (2.5σ) cores detected, no broad alkali wings, no broad [[H2O]] absorption — interpreted as a **pervasive haze of submicrometre condensate grains** spanning at least five atmospheric scale heights. The same dust scenario also explains four otherwise-puzzling features of the [[secondary-eclipse-spectroscopy]] / phase-curve dataset: large 3.6 μm phase-curve amplitude, anomalously small hot-spot offset, near-blackbody mid-IR dayside emission, and the 8 μm/4.5 μm brightness ratio. Speculates that the HD 189733 b ↔ HD 209458 b "pM/pL" hot-Jupiter dichotomy may be **clear vs. dusty** rather than primarily about thermal inversions.

## Key findings
- **Panchromatic Rayleigh slope.** Transmission radius increases monotonically from 8 μm (R_p/R_⋆ ≈ 0.1546) to 0.3 μm (≈ 0.1581), well-described by σ ∝ λ⁻⁴ scattering at T ≈ 1300 K consistent with Rayleigh-Jeans limb temperature.
- **Narrow Na D + tentative K cores.** Pixel-by-pixel reanalysis of ACS in a 90 nm window around the K doublet shows a 2.5σ K signature near the line core consistent with the Sing et al. 2011 Na width; broad pressure-broadened wings absent. Reinforced by Huitson et al. 2012 (not yet ingested) Na detection in STIS medium-resolution data.
- **Featureless infrared.** WFC3 1.10–1.69 μm and NICMOS 1.4–2.5 μm transmissions both flatter than predicted from cloud-free models with H₂O; muted molecular features ≤ 1 atmospheric scale height. NICMOS spectroscopic data (Swain, Vasisht & Tinetti 2008; not ingested) deemed unreliable; F166N/F187N photometric points retained.
- **Eclipse + phase-curve features explained by dust.** Re-analysis of Knutson et al. 2012 (not ingested) Spitzer phase curves: the 3.6 μm amplitude exceeds 4.5/8/24 μm (1.7×), the hot-spot offset is anomalously small (16°), and the dayside emission is closer to a 1200 K blackbody than to molecular-band models. All four features fall out naturally if dust opacity moves the photosphere upward (lower pressure) at all wavelengths, with extra opacity at 3.6 μm relative to 4.5 μm.
- **Antigreenhouse temperature inversion.** Modeling the Heng et al. 2012 framework with γ = 10, ξ = 0.1 (scattering-dominated dust), Pont et al. derive a thermal-inversion T-P profile (~136 ± 14 K between the 3.6 μm and 4.5 μm photospheric pressures) **caused by visible-light absorption by dust** — distinct from the TiO/VO inversion mechanism invoked for [[HD-209458b]].
- **Unocculted spot framework.** Reference unocculted spot level 1–2 % (Strömgren b+y bandpass), spot temperature 4 250 ± 250 K, additional 0.3 %@8 μm scatter from unrecognized smaller spot crossings. Reference reduction includes spot-level uncertainty as a systematic error budget; final IR transit-depth uncertainties significantly larger than in earlier studies that ignored this.
- **Hot-Jupiter dichotomy reframed.** HD 189733 b vs. HD 209458 b may be the prototypes of two classes — *clear, low-albedo, transparent in the red* (HD 209458 b) vs. *dusty, blue-blocked, antigreenhouse-inverted* (HD 189733 b) — perhaps controlled by host-star UV activity (HD 189733 active, HD 209458 quiet) rather than purely temperature.

## Methods
Self-consistent transit re-fitting across 6 instruments, with **two distinct [[stellar-contamination]] corrections**: (i) the AC component (varying flux from spots rotating into and out of view), measured via Gaussian-process interpolation of the 5+ year APT b+y photometry monitoring of HD 189733 (Henry; Croll et al. 2007 MOST campaign cross-check); and (ii) the DC component (steady minimum spot dimming) — bracketed at 0–3 % for a smooth-spot distribution. MARCS T = 4 000 / 4 250 / 4 500 K spot models give per-instrument scaling factors c_λ (their Table 4) connecting AC corrections to actual transit-depth offsets. Occulted-spot residuals visible in every visible HST transit but no infrared crossings; an additional ±0.3 %@8 μm random uncertainty propagated for unrecognized infrared crossings. Extended-haze model fit at first order: Rayleigh-scattering grains with constant (≤ 0.1 μm) size cutoff over 5+ scale heights; settling-regime sub-region with grain size growing as a_max(p) ∝ p; cloud-deck below.

## Caveats & limitations
- "Weather" variability between transits not directly tested; spectrum constructed assuming intrinsic atmospheric transmission is constant within the observational uncertainties.
- Spot-temperature distribution treated as single-effective-temperature; "leopard skin" small-spot population (numerous, possibly cooler) would be invisible to occulted-crossing constraints and could shift the spectrum at the few-100 ppm level.
- One discrepant APT season excluded (mismatch between Gaussian-process interpolation and re-scaled HST/STIS+ACS comparison); flagged as possible epoch-to-epoch atmospheric variability or photometric calibration issue.
- HD 189733 stellar variability acquired during ~5 yr; if the spot pattern evolves (it does — post-2009 ground-based pattern is qualitatively different from 2007–2008), residual systematic uncertainties at the ~100 ppm level cannot be excluded.
- The haze + cloud-deck composition not constrained by the data; silicates (enstatite, forsterite, corundum, Fe), photochemical hydrocarbons / soot, all possible.
- Dusty interpretation is the simplest unifier of transmission + emission + phase-curve, but a clear-atmosphere alternative with non-equilibrium chemistry remains formally compatible with subsets of the data.

## Open questions / follow-ups
- Direct test of dust-vs.-clear via reflected-light albedo measurement (Berdyugina et al. 2008 polarimetric high-albedo claim not confirmed by Wiktorowicz 2009; both not ingested).
- Connection to brown-dwarf L → T spectral transition: HD 189733 b at T ≈ 1 200 K falls in the L–T transition zone where dust photospheric coverage drops dramatically; analogues among directly imaged young Jupiters around HR 8799 worth comparing.
- WFC3 [Wakeford et al. 2013] cross-check on the 1.4 μm water band: are muted features dust opacity, or is the underlying H₂O abundance subsolar?
- Population of hot Jupiters around active vs. quiet stars: does dust correlate with stellar UV / activity?
